ELECTRONICS #1

In this assignment, I will be completing an assignment about electrical circuits.

9.1 Solving Problems

Initially, we learned about the SI units (le système international d'unités), and how they are the best system ever. We learned how there are 7 base units and all the other units are derived from each other. Such as one Newton is derived from kgm/s2 and Power (Watt) is derived Joules per second which is derived from Newton meter. We also learned about SI prefixes, such as km means a thousand meters, MJ means one million Joules or mA means 0.001 Amps, etc.

Later, we learned about units for electricity and how they work. We learned about Volts, Current, and Resistance. We learned that how they resemble a water flowing through a pipe. The volt is the pressure of the water, the Current is the flow rate of the water, and the resistance is how much resistance there is on the pipe restricting the water flow. In our case water flow is the current, water pressure is Voltage and the pipe shape etc. is our resistance.

After learning the figurative idea behind Voltage, Current and Resistance we moved on to learning what they actually represent. Initially we learned about Resistance's symbol, derived unit, how it's written in SI units and meaning: "Resistance represents how much the charge is impeded." Then we repeated this step for Voltage and Current (Amperage) too. We learned that the Voltage represents the difference in electric potential energy, representing how much the charge wants to flow from one location to another, it shows the potential energy for charge, or Joules per Coulomb. We also learned that the Current (Amperage) represents the flow rate of charge, how fast it is flowing, charge per unit time, or Coulomb per second.

We also learned about how these 3 units are corelated according to Ohm's Law. Ohm's Law says that Voltage is equals to Current times Resistance. In other words, the potential difference in the circuit can be found by multiplying the flow rate of charges by how much the charge is impeded. Using basic Algebra we also learned that V=IR can be written as I=V/R or R=V/I.

We then learned about Power. we learned that the symbol of Power is P, the derived unit is Watt (W), as SI units it is represented as kgm2/s2 and Power represents the amount of energy spent per unit of time, or Joules per second.

Now that we learned about units and basic circuits, we moved on to applying our knowledge to some examples. we got the example down below with a 10V battery and 100 ohm resistor. We predicted the current of this circuit using Ohm's Law and we found 0.1 Amps or 100 mA. We then placed an Ampmeter to see if we were right and we were. Note that we also learned how to place Ampmeter and Voltmeter correctly to not blow up any equipment. We then used a different circuit to see how a Voltmeter works.

After solving some practice problems, we moved on to series and parallel circuits. In series circuits the resistance or the device is connected in series which makes the same current flow from each of the components and if the resistance differs in each component (resistor or LED, etc) then the voltage too will differ. We also learned how to calculate the total resistance in series circuits (just add them up). Moving on to Parallel circuits, we learned how this time the total voltage in the parallel section stays the same and the current divides according to the resistance of the components and the voltage. We learned how to calculate the resistance in a parallel circuit ( (1/R) = (1/r1) + (1/r2) + (1/r3) ).
